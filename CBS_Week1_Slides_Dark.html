Part 1 of 7

What ‘AI’ actually means

PLACEHOLDER

The word 'AI' covers many things

From your phone keyboard to ChatGPT — they’re all called AI

  • Spam filters in your email
  • Netflix and Spotify recommendations
  • Voice assistants like Siri and Alexa
  • Self-driving car navigation systems
  • ChatGPT and image generators
  • All of these are 'AI' — but they work very differently
PLACEHOLDER

The key insight

AI is not one thing.
It’s a family of techniques that share a common goal:
making computers do things that used to need human intelligence.
PLACEHOLDER

AI ⊃ Machine Learning ⊃ Deep Learning

A nested hierarchy

  • Artificial Intelligence (AI) — the broadest goal
  • Machine Learning (ML) — a subset of AI: systems that learn from data
  • Deep Learning (DL) — a subset of ML using neural networks with many layers
  • Generative AI is a subset of deep learning
  • Each layer narrows the scope but adds capability
PLACEHOLDER

The four pillars of AI

Different problems need different approaches

Rule-based

Hand-coded 'if-then' rules. Explainable but brittle. Used for tax software, legacy systems.

Machine learning

Learns patterns from data. Powers fraud detection, recommendation engines, credit scoring.

Deep learning

Multi-layer neural networks. Powers image recognition, speech, language understanding.

Generative AI

Specialised deep learning that produces new content. ChatGPT, Midjourney, Sora.

PLACEHOLDER

Part 2 of 7

What ‘learning’ actually means

PLACEHOLDER

An analogy

Imagine teaching a child to recognise dogs.
You don’t give them a definition.
You show them many dogs and say 'dog.'
Eventually they generalise.

That’s machine learning.
PLACEHOLDER

Three ways machines learn

Each fits different business problems

  • Supervised learning — learn from labelled examples ('this is fraud, this is not')
  • Unsupervised learning — find patterns without labels (customer segmentation)
  • Reinforcement learning — learn by trial and error with rewards (game playing, robotics)
  • Most business AI today is supervised learning
PLACEHOLDER

Supervised vs. unsupervised

The most common business distinction

Supervised

You have labelled data

  • Predict if a loan will default
  • Classify emails as spam or not
  • Forecast next quarter’s sales
  • Needs historical examples with answers

Unsupervised

You only have raw data

  • Group customers into segments
  • Detect unusual transactions
  • Find topics in documents
  • Useful when answers are unknown

Part 3 of 7

The ML workflow

PLACEHOLDER

From raw data to working model

Six stages — most projects fail in stages 1–2

  • 1. Define the problem and success metric
  • 2. Collect and clean the data
  • 3. Split into training, validation, and test sets
  • 4. Train the model on the training data
  • 5. Validate and tune (don’t touch test data yet)
  • 6. Deploy and monitor in production
PLACEHOLDER

The 80/20 reality

80% of an ML project is data work.
20% is the actual modelling.

If you only remember one thing from this session, remember this.
PLACEHOLDER

Why train/validation/test split matters

Preventing the model from 'cheating'

  • Training set — the model learns patterns from this
  • Validation set — used to tune the model during development
  • Test set — untouched until the very end, simulates real-world data
  • If you test on training data, you measure memorisation, not understanding
PLACEHOLDER

Overfitting

A student who memorises every practice question
but fails the real exam.

That’s what an overfit model does:
perfect on training data, useless on new data.
PLACEHOLDER

Part 4 of 7

Reading model performance

PLACEHOLDER

The confusion matrix

A 2x2 table that shows where the model is right and wrong

  • True positives — model said yes, answer was yes
  • True negatives — model said no, answer was no
  • False positives — model said yes, answer was no (false alarm)
  • False negatives — model said no, answer was yes (miss)
  • Different applications care about different errors
PLACEHOLDER

Two metrics that matter

Choose based on what error costs more

Precision

When the model says yes, how often is it right?

  • Of all flagged transactions, how many were actually fraud?
  • Important when false alarms are expensive
  • Example: spam filter — don’t block real emails

Recall

Of all real positives, how many did we catch?

  • Of all actual fraud, how much did we catch?
  • Important when missing cases is dangerous
  • Example: cancer screening — don’t miss tumours
PLACEHOLDER

The trade-off

You usually can’t maximise both precision and recall.

A bank fraud team might tolerate false alarms (low precision)
to catch every fraud case (high recall).

A spam filter does the opposite.
PLACEHOLDER

Other metrics you’ll hear about

Don’t memorise the formulas — just know what they tell you

  • Accuracy — percentage of correct predictions (misleading for imbalanced data)
  • F1 score — a balance between precision and recall
  • AUC-ROC — how well the model separates classes across thresholds
  • Always ask: which error is more costly here?
PLACEHOLDER

Part 5 of 7

Why data quality decides everything

PLACEHOLDER

Garbage in, garbage out

An ML model is only as good as the data it learned from.

Bad data → bad model.
No amount of clever algorithms fixes this.
PLACEHOLDER

Four ways data fails AI projects

All of these come back in Week 2 cases

Bias

Training data over-represents one group. The model learns the bias.

Volume

Too few examples to learn from. Model memorises instead of generalising.

Drift

The world changes after training. The model becomes stale.

Labelling errors

Wrong answers in the training data. The model learns wrong patterns.

PLACEHOLDER

Real example: Amazon’s hiring AI

We’ll come back to this in Week 3

  • Amazon trained an AI to screen CVs based on 10 years of past hires
  • Past hires were mostly male (the tech industry pattern)
  • The model learned to penalise female-coded words and women’s colleges
  • Project scrapped — the data encoded historical bias
  • The algorithm wasn’t broken. The data was.
PLACEHOLDER

Real example: COVID demand forecasting

A whole industry of models broke at the same time

  • Retail and supply chain models trained on years of stable patterns
  • March 2020 — buying behaviour changed overnight
  • Models kept predicting based on historical patterns
  • Result: stockouts, overproduction, billions in losses
  • This is data drift in action
PLACEHOLDER

Part 6 of 7

Common misconceptions

PLACEHOLDER

Things people often get wrong

Watch for these in vendor pitches

  • "More data is always better" — not if it’s biased or stale
  • "AI is objective" — it inherits the biases of its training data
  • "AI will keep improving on its own" — it won’t, without retraining
  • "The algorithm is the secret sauce" — the data is
  • "More accurate = better" — depends which kind of error
PLACEHOLDER

The honest summary

Machine learning is pattern matching at scale.

It’s powerful when patterns exist and are stable.
It’s dangerous when patterns change
or when the past doesn’t represent the future.

Part 4 of 5

Generative AI

PLACEHOLDER

The shift in 2022

For decades, AI mostly classified or predicted.

Then came ChatGPT, and AI started generating.
New text. New images. New code. New audio.
PLACEHOLDER

How an LLM generates text

The crucial mental model

  • An LLM is trained to predict the next word in a sequence
  • It does this billions of times on internet-scale text
  • When you give it a prompt, it generates one word at a time
  • Each word is the most probable next word given everything before
  • That’s it. There’s no reasoning, no understanding, no plan.
  • There’s only prediction — very, very good prediction.
PLACEHOLDER

Try it yourself

"The cat sat on the ___"

P(mat) = 0.31
P(roof) = 0.12
P(floor) = 0.09
…

An LLM picks one based on probability and continues.
PLACEHOLDER

Why this matters

Understanding the mechanism predicts the failure modes

  • LLMs can be confidently wrong — they predict, they don’t verify
  • They can’t do math reliably — prediction ≠ calculation
  • They don’t know what they don’t know
  • They make up sources, statistics, and case law
  • This is called 'hallucination' and it’s built into how they work
PLACEHOLDER

Business uses of generative AI

Where it actually helps and where it doesn’t

Works well

Drafting first versions of documents, summarising long texts, brainstorming ideas, translating language.

Works with care

Customer service (with guardrails), code assistance (with review), research (with verification).

Risky

Legal and medical advice, anything where being confidently wrong has consequences.

Doesn’t work

Tasks needing real-time data, exact calculations, or guaranteed factual accuracy.

Part 5 of 5

Agentic AI

PLACEHOLDER

From generative to agentic

Generative AI: you ask, it answers. One turn.
Agentic AI: you give a goal, it plans, acts, observes, adjusts.

The same underlying LLM — wrapped in a planning loop with tool access.
PLACEHOLDER

The fundamental difference

Why agentic is a category shift, not an upgrade

Generative AI

You ask → it answers

  • Single turn
  • Predicts the next token
  • Stateless — no memory between calls
  • Cannot take action in the world
  • You evaluate the output

Agentic AI

You ask → it plans → acts → adjusts

  • Multi-step
  • Uses tools (search, APIs, files)
  • Maintains state across steps
  • Takes real actions on real systems
  • You evaluate the outcome
PLACEHOLDER

How an agentic system actually works

The plan-act-observe-adjust loop

  • 1. User gives a goal: 'book me a flight to Paris next Tuesday'
  • 2. LLM plans the steps it needs to take
  • 3. LLM calls a tool (search flights, check calendar)
  • 4. LLM observes the result
  • 5. LLM adjusts its plan based on what it learned
  • 6. Repeat until the goal is achieved — or the agent gives up
PLACEHOLDER

Agentic AI — benefits

What it unlocks

End-to-end automation

Multi-step tasks complete without human intervention at every stage.

Adaptive to context

Can handle ambiguous goals and unexpected obstacles by replanning.

Tool integration

Uses existing software (APIs, databases, browsers) without custom integration code.

Faster knowledge work

Research, analysis, drafting, and routine decision-making at machine speed.

PLACEHOLDER

Agentic AI — business use cases

Real and emerging applications

Customer service resolution

Agent reads ticket, checks order DB, issues refund, sends email — one workflow.

Financial research & reporting

Pulls earnings data, summarises filings, generates draft analyst report.

Coding & development

Reads codebase, identifies bug, writes fix, runs tests, opens pull request.

Compliance & due diligence

Reviews contracts, flags risks, cross-checks against regulations, drafts memos.

PLACEHOLDER

Agentic AI — pitfalls

Where this gets dangerous fast

Compounding errors

A 95%-accurate step run 10 times is 60% accurate end-to-end. Long workflows fail silently.

Real-world side effects

Agents that act on real systems can issue refunds, send emails, or delete files in error.

Prompt injection

Malicious instructions in fetched content can hijack the agent’s tools.

Accountability gap

When an autonomous agent makes a bad decision, who is responsible? Legal frameworks are unclear.

PLACEHOLDER

The rule of thumb

Use a human-in-the-loop wherever the action is
irreversible, costly, or regulated.

Fully autonomous agents are best for low-stakes,
reversible, internal-only workflows.

A new framing

Without data lineage

Core concepts

PLACEHOLDER

The vocabulary

Five terms that name the disciplines you just saw

Data governance

The policies, roles, and processes for managing data as a corporate asset.

Data stewardship

The day-to-day work of maintaining data quality and resolving issues.

Master data management

PLACEHOLDER

Why metadata is the foundation

The unglamorous thing that makes everything else possible

  • If you don’t know what data you have, you cannot use it
  • If you don’t know where it came from, you cannot trust it
  • If you don’t know what it means, you cannot apply it correctly
  • Metadata answers all three questions
  • Data catalogues are the modern way to manage metadata at scale
PLACEHOLDER

The DAMA-DMBOK framework

DAMA International publishes the Data Management Body of Knowledge.
It defines 11 knowledge areas for data management.
Used worldwide as the reference framework.

You don’t need to memorise it — just know it exists
and that governance sits at its centre.

Part 4 of 6

The data-to-AI pipeline

PLACEHOLDER

From raw data to deployed AI

Eight stages — each one a place where governance lives or fails

  • 1. Source systems — where data is generated
  • 2. Ingestion — pulling data into a central store
  • 3. Quality gates — checking for completeness and accuracy
  • 4. Transformation — cleaning, joining, reshaping
  • 5. Feature store — versioned features ready for ML
  • 6. Training — building the model
  • 7. Validation — testing before release
  • 8. Deployment & monitoring — the production system
PLACEHOLDER

Where governance lives

At every stage, the same questions:
Who owns this data?
Who can access it?
Is it good enough quality?
Is the lineage documented?

No answers → no production AI.
PLACEHOLDER

Two ends, one chain

Why the whole pipeline matters

  • Most organisations focus on the model — stage 6
  • Most failures happen in stages 1–3 — long before training
  • A weakness anywhere in the chain compromises everything downstream
  • Governance is what holds the whole chain together
  • This is why '80% of an AI project is data work' is not an exaggeration

Part 5 of 6

Centralised vs. federated governance

PLACEHOLDER

Two operating models

Once you accept that governance is essential,
the next question is how to organise it.

Two dominant models. Both work. Both fail differently.
PLACEHOLDER

Two governance models

Each fits a different organisation

Centralised (CDO)

Single chief data officer, central team

  • One authority, one set of standards
  • Consistency across the organisation
  • Slower to adapt to local needs
  • Better fit for highly regulated industries
  • Bottleneck risk as the organisation grows

Federated (data mesh)

Domain teams own their data products

  • Each business unit owns its data
  • Faster, more agile, more locally relevant
  • Harder to enforce consistency
  • Better fit for technology-led organisations
  • Requires a strong central platform team
PLACEHOLDER

Most large organisations are hybrid

Central principles, local execution.
A central CDO sets policies.
Domain teams implement them.
It’s messy in practice — but it works.

Part 6 of 6

Regulation & discussion

PLACEHOLDER

How regulation forces the issue

GDPR and the EU AI Act make governance non-optional

  • GDPR Article 5 — data must be accurate, up to date, and minimised
  • GDPR Article 25 — privacy by design and by default
  • EU AI Act Article 10 — high-risk AI must have data quality, documentation, traceability
  • Right to explanation — you must be able to explain how a model made a decision
  • All of these require governance — you cannot bolt them on later

Part 1 of 6

Why quantum is different

PLACEHOLDER

Setting expectations

Quantum computing will not replace your laptop.
It will not run ChatGPT faster.
It won’t make Excel spreadsheets quicker.

It does something fundamentally different.
PLACEHOLDER

Classical vs. quantum: the core difference

One bit at a time vs. many possibilities at once

  • A classical bit is either 0 or 1. Always one or the other.
  • A quantum bit (qubit) can be in a combination of 0 and 1 — until you measure it
  • With many qubits, you can explore many possible states at the same time
  • When you measure, you get one answer — but the algorithm steered toward useful answers
  • This is only an advantage for very specific problem types
PLACEHOLDER

The coin-flip analogy

Imagine spinning a coin on a table.
While it’s spinning, it’s neither heads nor tails — it’s both, in a sense.

When the coin lands, you get one answer.

A qubit 'spins' in a similar way — until measured.
PLACEHOLDER

Part 2 of 6

Qubits, superposition, entanglement

PLACEHOLDER

Three quantum concepts you need

No equations — just the ideas

  • Superposition — a qubit can be in multiple states at once
  • Entanglement — two qubits can be linked so measuring one affects the other
  • Interference — quantum algorithms cancel out wrong answers and amplify right ones
  • Together, these let quantum computers explore many possibilities in parallel
PLACEHOLDER

Why this matters

A classical computer with N bits can be in 1 of 2^N states.
A quantum computer with N qubits can be in a combination of all 2^N states.

For 300 qubits, that’s more states than there are atoms in the universe.
PLACEHOLDER

But there’s a catch

The reason you don’t have a quantum laptop

  • Qubits are extremely fragile — the slightest disturbance ruins computation
  • Most quantum computers operate near absolute zero (−273°C)
  • Errors compound quickly — you need error correction
  • We’re still in the 'noisy intermediate-scale' era (NISQ)
  • True large-scale fault-tolerant quantum computing is years away
PLACEHOLDER

Part 3 of 6

When does quantum help?

PLACEHOLDER

The honest answer

Quantum computing offers speedup
for a small number of specific problem types.

For everything else, classical computers are better
— and will remain so.
PLACEHOLDER

Where quantum could matter

The four main application areas

Optimization

Portfolio optimization, route planning, scheduling. Where you must search many combinations.

Simulation

Chemistry, materials, drug discovery. Simulating quantum systems is what quantum is built for.

Cryptography

Breaking RSA encryption (eventually). Also enabling new forms of secure communication.

Machine learning

Some specific ML problems may benefit. Most won’t.

PLACEHOLDER

Finance applications worth knowing

What banks and asset managers are exploring

  • Portfolio optimization — finding the best mix of assets under constraints
  • Monte Carlo simulation — valuing complex derivatives faster
  • Risk modelling — simulating many scenarios in parallel
  • Fraud detection — finding patterns in transaction graphs
  • All still experimental — no production systems beating classical at scale yet
PLACEHOLDER

Part 4 of 6

The realistic timeline

PLACEHOLDER

Where we are now

Snapshot

  • IBM, Google, IonQ, and others have working quantum computers
  • Largest systems have ~1000 qubits but high error rates
  • Quantum advantage demonstrated for narrow benchmark problems
  • No commercial application has beaten classical at scale yet
  • Active research, real progress, but no production systems
PLACEHOLDER

Why timelines keep slipping

A pattern worth recognising

  • 2015 forecasts: quantum advantage by 2020. Didn’t happen.
  • 2020 forecasts: useful quantum computers by 2025. Slipping.
  • Engineering challenges harder than expected (decoherence, error correction)
  • Breakthroughs in classical computing close some quantum advantages
  • Realistic horizon: 5–15 years for narrow useful applications, longer for general use
PLACEHOLDER

The CEO question

'Should we invest in quantum computing now?'

For most businesses: no, not directly.
Watch the field. Have someone tracking it.
Don’t bet the strategy on it.

Part 5 of 6

Post-quantum cryptography

PLACEHOLDER

The crypto threat

One area where you should pay attention now

  • Most internet encryption (RSA, ECC) relies on factoring being hard
  • Shor’s algorithm — a quantum algorithm — can factor large numbers efficiently
  • Once a sufficiently large quantum computer exists, current encryption breaks
  • We’re not there yet — but the threat is real and approaching
  • This is why governments and banks are preparing now
PLACEHOLDER

"Harvest now, decrypt later"

An adversary can intercept encrypted data today,
store it,
and decrypt it years from now
when quantum computers are powerful enough.

For secrets that need to stay secret for decades,
the threat is already real.
PLACEHOLDER

What organisations should do

The post-quantum migration

  • NIST has standardised post-quantum cryptography algorithms (2024)
  • Inventory: which systems use what kinds of encryption?
  • Prioritise: long-lived secrets first (medical records, state secrets)
  • Plan migration: this will take 5–10 years for large organisations
  • Don’t wait until the quantum computer exists — it’s already too late by then

Part 5 of 6

Common misconceptions to address

PLACEHOLDER

Misconceptions I see every year

Things that come up reliably

"Bigger model = better"

Often false. The right model for the job, with good data, beats a giant generic model.

"AI is objective"

AI inherits the biases in its training data. 'Objective' is the opposite of how it works.

"Quantum will replace AI"

They solve different problems. Quantum won’t make ML faster or more accurate.

"Governance is bureaucracy"

Governance is what makes AI possible at scale. Without it, you have prototypes, not products.

PLACEHOLDER

More misconceptions

"AI explains itself"

Most modern AI (deep learning, LLMs) is a black box. Explainability is hard — and often inadequate.

"Data privacy and AI are incompatible"

They’re in tension, but not incompatible. Federated learning, differential privacy exist.

"Agentic AI = AGI"

Agentic AI is multi-step LLMs with tools. It’s not artificial general intelligence.

"AI will plateau soon"

Predicted many times. Hasn’t happened yet. Don’t bet on it either way.
